# The Placement Assessment — Ideas for Your Review

*A short summary of the thinking behind the online placement quiz, with example problems at each level. This is a draft for your feedback — nothing here is final.*

---

## The heart of it

The chart already asks the right question at every level: **can the child actually do the work of this level yet?** The online version simply lets the child *show* us instead of asking the parent to guess. A child works a handful of real problems, and the quiz finds the first place where the work gets ahead of them. That spot is where they begin — not where they fell short, but where they're ready to grow.

Three convictions shaped every problem:

**1. Understanding shows up in word problems, not bare drill.**
A child can memorize that 7 × 8 = 56 and still have no idea when to multiply. Meaning lives in the story — in knowing *which* operation a situation calls for. So the assessment leans toward word problems (about 60% of what a child sees), because that's where we find out whether the math is truly theirs or just recited. This mirrors the program's own conviction that math should be *meaningful* — concept before symbol.

**2. A number sentence should be something to think through, not just fill in.**
Alongside the plain problems, the computation questions use open sentences with a missing piece:

> 25 = 15 + ______     15 × ______ = 60     300 − ______ = 120

These ask more than "compute the answer." To solve `300 − ______ = 120`, a child has to *reason backward* — to understand what subtraction is doing, not just crank a procedure. It's the same habit of mind that later becomes algebra, and it's woven in from the very first level.

**3. A placement test should never feel like a test.**
There's no timer, no score, no red X's. When a child doesn't know something, they tap "I don't know yet" and move on — no pressure to guess. A lucky guess would only push them into a book that's too hard, so honesty is built into the design. The child never learns whether they "passed" a section; they just do a few problems, hear "nice work," and do a few more.

## What the problems look like at each level

*A sampling — not the full set. Each level mixes real-world word problems with a couple of computation problems, at least one of them an open sentence.*

### Level 1 — numbers 0–20
- *Maria has 8 apples. She gives 3 to her brother. How many apples does she have left?*
- *Sam has 6 marbles. A friend gives him 7 more. How many does he have now?*
- 9 + 6 = ______
- **20 = 12 + ______**

### Level 2 — borrowing & carrying, 0–99
- *A farmer collects 47 eggs on Monday and 36 on Tuesday. How many eggs in all?*
- *A book has 82 pages. Nora has read 45. How many pages are left?*
- 52 − 27 = ______
- **80 − ______ = 53**

### Level 3 — 0–999, multiplication & division facts, fractions
- *There are 7 baskets with 8 apples in each. How many apples altogether?*
- *A pizza is cut into 4 equal pieces and you eat 3. What fraction of the pizza did you eat?*
- 304 − 178 = ______
- **15 × ______ = 60**

### Level 4 — larger numbers, multi-digit multiplication, comparing fractions
- *A stadium holds 34 rows of 216 seats. How many seats in all?*
- *Which is more of a pizza — 2⁄3 or 3⁄4?*
- 412,305 − 178,426 = ______
- **______ × 216 = 7,344**

### Level 5 — big numbers, long multiplication & division, fraction operations
- *A bakery uses 3⁄4 cup of sugar per batch. How much sugar for two batches?*
- *34,258 seeds are shared equally among 7 fields. How many seeds per field?*
- 245 × 1,338 = ______
- **______ × 1,338 = 327,810**

### Level 6 — decimals, long division, ratio & percent
- *A board is 2.75 meters long. You cut off 1.4 meters. How long is the piece that's left?*
- *A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk. With 6 cups of flour, how much milk?*
- 4.6 × 2.3 = ______
- **7.2 − ______ = 3.35**

### Level 7 — the doorway to algebra
- *Think of a number. Five more than that number… which expression shows it?*
- *One number plus another is 10; twice the first plus the second is 9. What is the second number?*
- **y + 4 = 9** — what is y?
- **3x + 2 = 11** — what is x?

## What I'd love your feedback on

- Does the **60% word / 40% computation** balance feel right for placing your students, or would you weight it differently?
- Do the **open-sentence problems** (`300 − ______ = 120`) match how you'd want reasoning tested at each level?
- Are the **story contexts** the kind you'd use — or too simple, too busy, off in tone?
- Anything a level is testing that it *shouldn't* for placement, or missing that it should?
